Overview & Introduction

  • infinitive

  • use of the infinitive

  • 3rd -io conjugation

  • A fundamental aspect of verbs is the infinitive form, which you will be using throughout your Latin journey. In this chapter, we will be exploring this foundational verb form along with a special, and final, verb conjugation.

Infinitive

  • 01.

    The infinitive is a form of the verb translating to “to (verb).”

  • 02.

    The form of the infinitive never changes, unlike the other verb forms we studied in the previous chapters.

  • 03.

    Each conjugation has a different infinitive form:

    • 1nd Conjugation

    • parō

    • parā-re

    • to prepare

    • 2nd Conjugation

    • moneō

    • monē-re

    • to warn

    • 3nd Conjugation

    • regō

    • reg-ere

    • to rule

    • 4nd Conjugation

    • audiō

    • audī-re

    • to hear

    Note that the stem vowels are different for each conjugation: this is the primary method of identifying a verb’s conjugation.
    1. 1st conjugation: ā
    2. 2nd conjugation: ē
    3. 3rd conjugation: e
    4. 4th conjugation: ī

  • 04.

    sum, being an irregular verb, has a special infinitive:

    sum                   esse                   to be

  • 05.

    Like in English, the infinitive is used with verbs such as:

    cupiō I desire to, want to:

    iuvare cupimus

    We want to play.

    dēbeō I ought to, must:

    labōrāre dēbētis

    You ought to work

    iubeoōI order (someone) to:

    magister puerōs labōrāre iubet

    The master orders the boys to work.

    cōnstituōI decide to:

    magister fābulam nārrāre cōnstituit

    The master decides to tell a story.

Use of the Infinitive

  • 01.

    The infinitive has many helpful uses.

    As mentioned in the last section, we can identify the conjugation of a verb using its infinitive.

  • 02.

    We can also identify the stem of the verb using the infinitive:

    By removing the -re from the infinitive of a verb, or the -ere from the infinitive of a 3rd conjugation verb, we can find the stem of the verb:

    • 1st Conjugation

    • parā-re

    • parā

    • 2st Conjugation

    • monē-re

    • monē

    • 3st Conjugation

    • reg-ere

    • reg

    • 4st Conjugation

    • audī-re

    • audī

    The only thing to watch out for is that the length of the stem vowel (ā, ē, ī) may need to be changed depending on the ending of the verb, as we discussed in Chapter 4.

  • 03.

    By finding the stem through the infinitive, we can easily conjugate verbs.

  • 04.

    Thus, from this point forward, verb entries in the vocabulary lists will be in the form of:

    parō, parāre I prepare

    moneō, monēreI warn

    regō, regereI rule

    audiō, audīre I hear

3rd -io

  • 01.

    Besides the regular four conjugations, there is a special conjugation ending in -iō which behaves like 3rd conjugation verbs in some forms and 4th conjugation verbs in others, eg. capiō (“I take”). These are called the 3rd conjugation -io verbs.

    • 3rd conjugation -io

      capiō

    • compare

    • 4th conjugation

      audiō

    • capi-ō

    • audi-ō

    • cap-is

    • aduī-s

    • cap-it

    • adui-t

    • cap-imus

    • aduī-mus

    • cap-itis

    • aduī-tis

    • capi-unt

    • adui-nt

    • infinitive

      cap-ere

    • aud-īre

    (the endings underlined are those of 3rd conjugation verbs, such as reg-ō)

  • 02.

    Summarizing these similarities:

    3rd conjugation -io verbs take 4th conjugation verb forms for:

    First person singular

    Third person plural

    3rd conjugation -io verbs take 3rd conjugation verb forms for all of the other endings:

    Second person singular

    Third person singular

    First person plural

    Second person plural

    Third person plural

    Infinitive

  • 03.

    Thus, in the vocabulary lists, a 3rd conjugation -io verbs will read as:

    capiō, capere I take

    Organizing the endings of the 3rd conjugation -io:

Optional Reading

  • Education

  • Most Roman citizens were literate and their children received a primary education at the local schools from the ages of six or seven to eleven or twelve. Here they were taught reading, writing, and elementary arithmetic. The evidence suggests that girls, as well as boys, attended these schools, though they did not, as far as we know, go on to the secondary or “grammar” schools. In our story we make both Quintus and Horatia go to the school of Flavius for their primary education but his father takes the boy away to Rome for his secondary education.

    A wealthy family would appoint a slave, often a Greek, as a kind of personal tutor who would take a child to and from school. He might also act as a language assistant in Greek. The two of them would be accompanied by another slave who carried the child’s books. In country towns, schoolchildren went to school on their own carrying their satchels and taking along the schoolmaster's pay once a month. But when Quintus went to school in Rome, his father took him there and sat in on his lessons.
    In primary education, i.e. the first three or four years, the three “R”s were hammered into boys and girls with a syllabus of reading and writing in both Latin and Greek. The pupils would write their lessons on tablets (tabulae) using a stylus. They would also learn elementary arithmetic using an abacus. Horace poked fun at math lessons in his poetry:
    Roman boys learn how to divide a penny by a hundred with long calculations.
    “Tell us, son of Albinus, what do you end up with if a twelfth is taken away from five twelfths? Can you say?”
    “A third.”
    “Well done! You’ll end up a millionaire. What does it come to if you add a twelfth?”
    “A half.”
    Is it any surprise that when we drag our children through these off- putting financial calculations they can’t find the inspiration for poetry?

    For the most part, the lessons were boring and unpleasant. Pupils sat on uncomfortable benches or chairs, often in noisy surroundings. They endlessly recited the alphabet both forwards and backwards, as well as chanting their multiplication tables again and again. Teaching started very early, soon after dawn, and a Roman poet called Martial complained violently at being woken up by the noise:
    Why can’t you stay out of our lives, you cursed schoolmaster, a man hateful to boys and girls alike? The crested cocks have not yet broken the silence of the night. Already you are making a noise with your cruel voice and your thwacks.

    After a break for lunch, lessons probably started up again. There would be a holiday every eighth day, short breaks in the winter and spring, and a very long vacation in the summer.

    Boys would move on to another school around the age of eleven or twelve for their secondary education. Here they would learn grammar or literature. Greek and Greek literature were an important part of the syllabus. All educated Romans were bilingual. Though they had conquered Greece, they still recognized the greatness of Greek literature. As Horace himself wrote, “Conquered Greece conquered its wild conqueror and brought the arts to rustic Italy.”

    At the secondary stage arithmetic, geometry, music, and astronomy were studied as minor subjects. The curriculum was not wide. There were medical schools in Greece but in Italy, there was no scientific education at all. When they were about sixteen, upper-class Roman boys went on for their tertiary education to a teacher of rhetoric who would teach them through public lectures. From him, they received a thorough training in speaking and arguing, and this was good preparation for a career in politics.

    After girls had dropped out of education at school after the first (primary) stage, they learned needlework, dancing, singing, and lyre-playing at home.
