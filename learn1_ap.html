  • The first step to learning any foreign language is to understand its alphabet and pronunciations. Latin sounded very much like modern Italian or Spanish, but its alphabet is almost exactly the same as the English alphabet : “it has 24 letters, all 26 letters of the English alphabet except j and w.” - bold “j” and “w.”

  • Today, Latin is rarely spoken outside of religious contexts; regardless, learning its pronunciation can assist your subvocalization (the practice of saying words in your head while reading) and can make your learning journey more enjoyable and dynamic. Who knows, maybe you will meet a like-minded Latin scholar one day and converse in Latin!

Consonants

  • c is always hard, as in cat (never soft as in nice).

  • g is always hard, as in God (except when it is followed by n: gn is sounded ngn, as in hangnail,
    so magnus is pronounced mangnus).

  • h is always sounded, as in hope.

  • i is used as a consonant as well as a vowel: as a consonant, it sounds like English y; so Latin iam is pronounced yam.

    it is a consonant when a word starts with i and a vowel or between two vowels.

  • q is never found except when followed by u, sounded as in English quick.

  • r is rolled, as in Italian or Spanish, and always sounded.

    However, if you have trouble rolling the r (as many people do), pronounce it regularly.

  • s is always soft, as in sit (never like z, as in rose).

  • v is pronounced like English w; so Latin vidi sounds weedee.

  • The other consonants are pronounced like their English equivalents.

  • Where double consonants occur, as in sitting, both consonants are pronounced; so ille is pronounced il-le
    (l is sounded twice).

Vowels

The five vowels each have a long and a short version:

  • a short, as in English cup.

    ā long, as in English father.

  • e short, as in English pet.

    ē long, as in English aim.

  • i short, as in English dip.

    ī long, as in English deep.

  • o short, as in English pot.

    ō long, as in English foal.

  • u short, as in English put.

    ū long, as in English fool.

  • To make pronunciation earlier, we have put a long mark (ā, ē, ī, ō, ū), or a macron, over all long vowels;
    all vowels without a macron are short.

Diphthongs

Diphthongs are two vowels making one sound.

  • ae, as in English high (pronounced “aye”).

  • au, as in English how.

  • ei, as in English eight.

  • eu, e-u (not a proper diphthong; both vowels are sounded).

  • oe, as in English boy.

  • ui, u-i (both vowels are sounded).

  • For pairs of non-diphthong vowels, both vowels are sounded separately.
