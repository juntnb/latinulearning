  • Before we tackle Latin head-on, we have to understand some of the basics of the language. Thankfully, Latin and English grammar have many similarities, and we will be learning about a few of the key differences.

Grammar Basics

  • 01.

    In Latin, the verb often comes at the end of a sentence.

    e.g. the translation of “I am a boy” is puer (a boy) sum (I am).

  • 01.

    Latin has no word for “the” (definite article) or “a” (indefinite article)

    You must supply these in the English translation as the context requires.

  • 01.

    Latin does not always express the subject.

    e.g. the verb laborat by itself can mean “he/she/it works.”

Sentence Structures

Below are the three sentence structures you will encounter:

  • 01.

    (subject) (verb)

    e.g. Scintilla laborat | “Scintilla is working”

    In sentences of this structure, the verb (laborat) describes the action of the sentence, and the subject (Scintilla) tells you
    who is performing the action.

  • 02.

    (subject) is (noun/adjective)

    e.g. Horatia est puella | “Horatia is a girl”

    e.g. Horatia fessa est | “Horatia is tired”

    In sentences of this structure, the verb (est) does not describe an action but simply joins the subject (Horatia) to
    the completing word: “Horatia is...” To complete this sentence, a completing word (called a “subjective complement”) is required.
    This sentence structure doesn’t abide by the “verb at the end of a sentence’ generalization.

  • 03.

    (subject) (object) (verb)

    e.g. puella Scientillam salutat | “Scientilla calls Horatia”

    This sentence structure will be explored in the following two chapters and is the primary sentence structure you will encounter.

  • Later, prepositions, relative clauses, and other aspects will be included in sentences, but all can be traced back to these three parent sentence structures.

Noun Genders

  • ●

    In Latin, nouns have genders, like how in French and Spanish and other modern languages, nouns are either masculine
    or feminine in gender.

  • ●

    While Latin nouns indicating male beings are naturally masculine (e.g. filius, “son”) and those indicating female beings
    are feminine (e.g. filia, “daughter”), the gender of most other nouns was a grammatical concept, not a natural one
    (e.g. ager, “field” is masculine; cena, “dinner,” is feminine), and so a noun’s gender must be memorized
    as part of the vocabulary entry.

  • ●

    Some nouns are neuter in gender, neither masculine nor feminine.

  • ●

    Certain nouns have a common gender, meaning that the noun may be either masculine or feminine,
    according to the sex of the particular subject

    - The word parens (“parent”) can either be a masculine or feminine noun depending on which parent is being referred to.

  • ●

    Neuter nouns will not be used regularly until Chapter 9, and common gender nouns are uncommon,
    but be aware of their existence.

    (subject) (verb)

    03.

    (subject) (object) (verb)

  • There are three genders in Latin: masculine, feminine, and neuter. There is also a combined gender that can be masculine
    or feminine, depending on the context.

Optional Reading

  • Quintus

  • Quintus, the hero of our story, actually existed. His full name was Quintus Horatius Flaccus and in English, he is usually called Horace.
    He was born on December 8, 65 B.C.E., in Venusia, a large town in the wild area of Apulia in south-east Italy. His father, whom he loved and admired, was a freedman, somebody who had been a slave and then been given his freedom; he was an auctioneer's agent who owned a small farm. But we know nothing at all about any other relatives. We have invented the name Scintilla for Quintus’ mother and given him a sister called Horatia.

    In telling the story of Quintus we have made up many details. But much of our story is true. We know from his own poetry that his father did not send him to the local school—though in our story we pretend that he did go there for a while—but “he dared to take me to Rome as a boy to be taught the arts which any father from the top levels of society would have his own sons taught.” In Rome, Flaccus took him to what was perhaps the best school, that of Orbilius. After this, he may have studied rhetoric, the art of public speaking, which was the usual form of tertiary education. Finally, when he was about twenty, his father sent him to the Academy in Athens, the most famous university of the ancient world, where he studied philosophy. No wonder Horace always speaks of him with gratitude and affection.

    At the age of twenty-one, he ended his career as a student in Athens and joined the army of Marcus Brutus, the leading assassin of Julius Caesar. Thus when he was twenty-two he fought as a military tribune—a surprisingly high rank—and even commanded a legion on the losing side in one of the bloodiest battles of the ancient world, at Philippi in north-east Greece (42 B.C.E.).

    After this humiliation, he returned to Rome, became a clerk in the Treasury, and started to write poetry. This led him to move into literary circles where he met Virgil, the greatest of the Roman poets. Virgil introduced him to Maecenas, one of the most powerful men of the time and a great patron of the arts. Maecenas became a close friend and presented him with a farm in the Sabine hills near Rome, and his support allowed Horace to devote himself entirely to writing poetry.
